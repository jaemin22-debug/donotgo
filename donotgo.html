English Poetry Reading Quiz

Part I. William Butler Yeats — The Lake Isle of Innisfree
1. Read the following stanza. Which of the five senses does the imagery in the phrase "bee-loud glade" primarily appeal to?
I will arise and go now, and go to Innisfree, And a small cabin build there, of clay and wattles made: Nine bean-rows will I have there, a hive for the honeybee, And live alone in the bee-loud glade.
2. Read the following stanza. What does the metaphorical expression "the veils of the morning" refer to?
And I shall have some peace there, for peace comes dropping slow, Dropping from the veils of the morning to where the cricket sings; There midnight's all a glimmer, and noon a purple glow, And evening full of the linnet's wings.
3. Read the following stanza. What reality about the speaker's current situation is explicitly revealed in the final two lines?
I will arise and go now, for always night and day I hear lake water lapping with low sounds by the shore; While I stand on the roadway, or on the pavements gray, I hear it in the deep heart's core.
Part II. Dylan Thomas — Do Not Go Gentle into That Good Night
4. Read the following stanza. What does the expression "that good night" metaphorically represent?
Do not go gentle into that good night, Old age should burn and rave at close of day; Rage, rage against the dying of the light.
5. Read the following stanza. In line 11, what does the pronoun "it" refer to in the phrase "they grieved it on its way"?
Wild men who caught and sang the sun in flight, And learn, too late, they grieved it on its way, Do not go gentle into that good night.
6. Read the following stanza. Which rhetorical device or figure of speech is primarily utilized in the juxtaposed expression "Curse, bless, me now"?
And you, my father, there on the sad height, Curse, bless, me now with your fierce tears, I pray. Do not go gentle into that good night. Rage, rage against the dying of the light.
